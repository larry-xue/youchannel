# YouChannel: What We Built, Why We Built It, and Why We Stopped

<ProductFrame>

YouChannel was an open-source product experiment: take YouTube videos that learners
already care about, analyze them with AI, and turn them into interactive language
practice. It was not a landing-page concept. It had a TanStack Start app, Supabase
schema, YouTube OAuth, Gemini analysis, live voice sessions, shadowing drills,
quotas, background jobs, and an admin console.

</ProductFrame>

<Callout title="Short version" tone="summary">

We built YouChannel because authentic video is one of the best language-learning
inputs, but it is usually trapped in passive watching. We stopped because the
learning loop was promising, while the operating model around model cost, platform
dependency, quota management, and real-time voice quality was too heavy for the
stage of the project.

</Callout>

## The product thesis

The starting belief was simple: learners do not only need more lessons. They need
better ways to practice with material they already want to watch.

Traditional learning products often begin with curriculum. YouChannel began with
source material:

- a YouTube playlist,
- a video the learner selected,
- a transcript or model-derived understanding,
- an AI partner that could explain, roleplay, quiz, and converse,
- a practice loop that produced shadowing drills and progress history.

The product was trying to close a gap between **authentic input** and **active
practice**.

## What we built

<StackGrid>
  <StackCard title="1. Library and import">
    YouTube OAuth connected a user's account. The app read playlist videos and
    normalized metadata into a Supabase-backed library.
  </StackCard>
  <StackCard title="2. Video analysis">
    A jobs service accepted video metadata, inserted records, and queued Gemini
    analysis jobs for summaries, scenes, speakers, timestamps, and transcripts.
  </StackCard>
  <StackCard title="3. Live practice">
    Gemini Live powered real-time speaking sessions with voice, resumption handles,
    conversation history, and a profile system for personalization.
  </StackCard>
  <StackCard title="4. Shadowing and progress">
    Recent sessions produced short drills. Learners recorded attempts, received
    scores, and could track progress over time.
  </StackCard>
</StackGrid>

The shape was intentionally product-like. There was a user-facing application,
an operational backend, and separate API documentation for integrations.

That mattered because the prototype did not fail at the "can we wire this up?"
level. It reached the harder question: **is this the right product to operate?**

## Why we built it

There were three product motivations.

### 1. YouTube has the material learners actually want

Learners often already know which shows, creators, interviews, tutorials, or
talks they care about. The missing piece is not content discovery. The missing
piece is a bridge from content to practice.

### 2. AI can convert passive media into active context

With a good analysis layer, a video can become:

- a summary,
- a timestamped study guide,
- a speaker map,
- a vocabulary surface,
- a conversation partner,
- a source for speaking drills.

That is a meaningful shift. The learner is no longer only watching. They are
using the video as a practice environment.

### 3. Live voice makes language learning feel less theoretical

Typing about a video is useful. Speaking about it is different. Live voice made
the product feel closer to real practice, especially when paired with learner
preferences and post-session drills.

<Callout title="The good signal" tone="positive">

The strongest signal was not any single feature. It was the combined loop:
watch something real, ask about it, talk through it, repeat useful sentences,
then see progress.

</Callout>

## What became hard

The same things that made YouChannel interesting also made it expensive to
operate.

## The quota problem was not just billing

A language-learning product has to feel generous. A video-analysis product has
to control cost. Those two forces fight each other.

YouChannel added quota grants, usage events, cache refresh helpers, and admin
views because the system needed to know:

- how much video analysis a user could run,
- how long a single video could be,
- how much chat or live practice remained,
- when to refund quota after failed analysis,
- how to explain limits without making the product feel punitive.

That is normal product infrastructure, but it is also a sign. If the quota system
becomes central before the learning experience is proven, the project is already
carrying business-model weight.

## The platform-dependency problem

YouChannel depended on two external platforms in a deep way:

1. YouTube for content access.
2. Gemini for video understanding and live voice.

Neither dependency was wrong. Both were rational. But together they meant the
core experience was bounded by API policy, model availability, cost, latency,
and behavior that the product did not fully control.

For an experiment, that is acceptable. For a product promise, it needs a stronger
operational plan.

## Why we stopped

We stopped because continuing would have required turning YouChannel into an
operations-heavy product before the learning wedge was narrow enough.

The right next version would not have been "add more features." It would have
been one of these:

- focus only on shadowing from imported clips,
- focus only on live speaking with no YouTube dependency,
- focus only on video analysis as a developer API,
- focus only on a local BYOK tool for learners who bring their own model key.

Each narrower path could be good. The combined product was too broad.

<Callout title="The actual conclusion" tone="warning">

The idea was not worthless. The product was over-composed. It tried to combine
content import, AI analysis, live voice, assessment, quotas, and admin operations
before one wedge had earned the right to carry the rest.

</Callout>

## What remains useful

The project is still worth publishing because it contains implementation lessons:

- Supabase schema design for user-owned video data.
- RLS patterns around learning data and quotas.
- A background analysis service using Fastify and pg-boss.
- An OpenAPI ingestion endpoint for external video metadata.
- Gemini Live session handling, resumption, and voice-practice UX.
- Admin tooling for videos, analyses, users, jobs, and quota grants.
- A real example of when to archive a product instead of forcing a launch.

## What we would do differently

If we restarted, we would pick one wedge:

1. **BYOK local study tool**: no hosted AI cost, no billing layer, user controls
   the Gemini key.
2. **Shadowing-only product**: extract short speakable drills from user-selected
   clips and focus the entire UX around repetition.
3. **Video analysis API**: serve builders, not learners, and make the product a
   developer infrastructure component.
4. **Live speaking companion**: remove YouTube import entirely and optimize for
   conversation quality.

The project was a useful reminder that a good product idea can still be the wrong
bundle.

## Closing note

YouChannel is now best understood as an open-source product archive: a real build,
a useful system, and a clear stopping point.

That is not a failure mode. It is one of the healthier endings for a product
experiment.
